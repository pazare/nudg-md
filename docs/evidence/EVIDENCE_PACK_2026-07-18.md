NUDG MD Evidence Pack — evidence current through July 18, 2026. Internal grades (not formal GRADE): A authoritative guideline/RCT/systematic review; B peer-reviewed with indirectness/heterogeneity/bias/observational; C older canonical, simulation, single-center QI, or cost model; U unverified — never present as fact. Quoted lines ≤25 words. Quoted guideline lines do not transfer authority to different populations. Relative risks are not individual probabilities. WORKSTREAM 1 — Nutrition in oncology. Bottom line: the defensible trigger is NOT "wait for 5% loss": ESPEN monitors intake/weight/BMI from diagnosis; ASPEN uses validated screening tools before treatment and regularly after; >5% loss is the consensus cachexia DIAGNOSTIC criterion (with >2% + BMI<20 or low muscle variant), not a universal referral threshold. Distinguish: routine longitudinal screening → tool-based risk escalation (MST/MUST/NRS-2002/NUTRISCORE/PG-SGA-SF) → high-priority assessment (>5% loss etc.) → intervention evidence (counseling/supplements improve weight and some chemo tolerance; survival evidence insufficient/inconsistent).
NUT-01 (A): ESPEN 2021 practical guideline (Muscaritoli et al., Clin Nutr, DOI 10.1016/j.clnu.2021.02.005): "regularly evaluate nutritional intake, weight change, and body mass index (BMI), beginning with cancer diagnosis". 43 recommendations; strong recommendation, very-low evidence.
NUT-02 (A): ASPEN 2024 (Trujillo et al., JPEN, DOI 10.1002/jpen.2688): screening "recommended before treatment initiation and regularly thereafter, depending on treatment course." Tool thresholds: MNA ≤23.5, MST ≥2, MUST ≥1/≥2, NRS-2002 ≥2/≥3, NUTRISCORE ≥5, PG-SGA-SF ≥7/≥8. 20/738 studies.
NUT-03 (A): ASCO 2020 (Roeland et al., JCO, DOI 10.1200/JCO.20.00611): "Clinicians may refer patients with advanced cancer and loss of appetite and/or body weight to a registered dietitian". Low evidence, moderate strength.
NUT-04 (A): Cachexia consensus (Fearon et al., Lancet Oncol 2011, DOI 10.1016/S1470-2045(10)70218-7): "The agreed diagnostic criterion for cachexia was weight loss greater than 5%" (or >2% with BMI<20 or reduced muscle mass). Delphi consensus, not a trial.
NUT-05 (B): Colorectal RCT 2025 (Wang et al., Nutrition and Cancer, DOI 10.1080/01635581.2025.2449719), n=88: dose-intensity reduction 9.30% vs 25.89% (p=.02); regimen change 25.58% vs 53.33% (p<.01); delay 13.95% vs 35.56% (p=.019); cycle-8 physical function 72.71 vs 57.63. "Individualized nutritional interventions improved chemotherapy tolerance and QOL and reduced adverse effects in this patient cohort."
NUT-06 (B): ONS post-colorectal-surgery RCT (Tan et al., Clin Nutr 2021, DOI 10.1016/j.clnu.2020.05.038), 212 analyzed: chemo modification 21.2% vs 36.8% (p=.024); sarcopenia 28.6% vs 42.1% (p=.040); no weight/QoL effect. "chemotherapy modifications, such as delay, dose reduction, or termination, were significantly reduced in the ONS group".
NUT-07 (B): Counseling in metastatic CRC (van der Werf et al., Clin Nutr 2020, DOI 10.1016/j.clnu.2020.01.009), n=107: weight +1.7 kg (p=.045); muscle loss 30% vs 31% (p=.467) — "The proportion of patients with a clinically relevant decrease in skeletal muscle area of ≥6.0 cm2 did not differ"; PFS/OS p-values significant but magnitudes UNVERIFIED.
NUT-08 (B): High-protein meta-analysis (Orsso et al., AJCN 2024, DOI 10.1016/j.ajcnut.2024.08.016), 35 RCTs n=3701: weight MD +1.45 kg (95% CI 0.42–2.48, I²=80%); no HRQoL difference. "Patients who received high-protein supplementation lost less body weight than controls".
NUT-09 (U): Evidence gap: no RCT verified restricted to colon-cancer/FOLFOX patients with ≥5% loss reporting interruptions, dose reductions, weight, QoL, survival together. UNVERIFIED.
Card spec: numbers first (weight change %, MST score, symptoms); why it matters (consensus cachexia threshold exceeded; RCTs suggest fewer chemo modifications; survival uncertain); workflow (dietitian assessment, symptom review, muscle/function assessment, monitoring). NEVER "improves survival", NEVER "ASCO mandates referral at 5%".

WORKSTREAM 2 — NSAIDs in CKD. Bottom line: KDIGO 2024 supports stewardship, not a universal every-NSAID prohibition at eGFR 30–60; the defensible high-priority trigger combines renal function + ACEi/ARB + diuretic + acute illness/dehydration/age/duration.
CKD-01 (A): KDIGO 2024 Practice Point 4.1.3 (Kidney Int, DOI 10.1016/j.kint.2023.10.018): "Review and limit the use of over-the-counter medicines and dietary or herbal remedies that may be harmful for people with CKD."
CKD-02 (A): 2020 joint guideline (Szeto et al., Gut, DOI 10.1136/gutjnl-2019-319300), grade 1C: avoid NSAIDs in "patients with moderate CKD (eGFR 30–59 mL/min/1.73 m2) receiving ACE inhibitors, ARBs or diuretics."
CKD-03 (B): Triple-whammy cohort (Lapi et al., BMJ 2013, DOI 10.1136/bmj.e8525), n=487,372: triple-therapy AKI RR 1.31 (1.12–1.53); first 30 days RR 1.82 (1.35–2.46). "The risk was greatest at the start of treatment."
CKD-04 (B): Lab-defined AKI (Dreischulte et al., Kidney Int 2015, DOI 10.1038/ki.2015.101), n=78,379: triple RR 1.64 (1.25–2.14); "numbers needed to harm for 1 year treatment with NSAID of 158 vs. over 300" for dual.
CKD-05 (U): "KDIGO forbids every NSAID at every eGFR 30–60" is UNVERIFIED — do not claim.
Nudge spec: show the patient's facts (eGFR, active ACEi/diuretic, planned NSAID), "studies found approximately 31%–64% relative AKI increases for triple combinations, greatest early"; actions: reconsider, alternative, document necessity + duration, or monitoring plan. NEVER "64% chance of AKI".

WORKSTREAM 3 — CDS and alert fatigue. Bottom line: high override ≠ irrational clinicians; many overrides are appropriate. Effective CDS: specific, actionable, patient-linked, severity-tiered, interruption-sparing. Best evidence: accountable justification, active choice, peer comparison, modal only for true high severity.
CDS-01 (B): DDI override meta-analysis (Felisberto et al., Health Informatics J 2024, DOI 10.1177/14604582241263242), 570,776 prescriptions: "the overall prevalence of alert override by physicians was 90%" (95% CI 85.6–95.0, I²=100%).
CDS-02 (B): Override appropriateness review (Poly et al., JMIR Med Inform 2020, DOI 10.2196/15653), 23 studies: "The range of average override alerts was 46.2% to 96.2%."; appropriateness 29.4%–100%.
CDS-03 (C): Severity tiering (Paterno et al., JAMIA 2009, DOI 10.1197/jamia.M2808), 71,350 alerts: compliance 29% vs 10%; most-severe acceptance 100% vs 34%. "Tiered alerting by severity was associated with higher compliance rates of DDI alerts in the inpatient setting".
CDS-04 (A): Cluster-RCT (Meeker et al., JAMA 2016, DOI 10.1001/jama.2016.0275), 248 clinicians/47 practices: accountable justification DID −7.0 pts (−9.1 to −2.9); peer comparison DID −5.2 pts. "Mean antibiotic prescribing rates decreased from 23.2% at intervention start to 5.2% at intervention month 18 for accountable justification."
CDS-05 (A): Cluster-RCT (Adusumalli et al., JAMA Cardiology 2023, DOI 10.1001/jamacardio.2022.4373): clinician nudge +5.5 pts; combined +7.2; "The patient nudge alone did not change statin prescribing relative to usual care".
CDS-06 (C): Simulation (Scott et al., JAMIA 2011, DOI 10.1136/amiajnl-2011-000199), 720 tasks: modal OR 11.56 vs no alert; "modal alerts were over three times more effective than nonmodal alerts." Reserve modals for high severity.
Architecture: context strip (noninterruptive) / active-choice card (2–3 executable options) / interruptive modal (verified, immediate, high-severity only). Expose triggering facts, evidence date+source, workflow, suppress/resolve, override reason. Measure appropriate acceptance/override/downstream action/burden, not raw click-through.

WORKSTREAM 4 — Clinician time and EHR navigation. Bottom line: visual source-linked brief, not another chat stream; emphasize what changed, one-click provenance, bounded action, batched nonurgent context.
TIME-01 (B): Overhage & McCallie, Ann Intern Med 2020, DOI 10.7326/M18-3684 (~100M encounters, 155k physicians): "Physicians spent an average of 16 minutes and 14 seconds per encounter using EHRs" — chart review 33%, documentation 24%, ordering 17%.
TIME-02 (B): Arndt et al., Ann Fam Med 2017, DOI 10.1370/afm.2121 (n=142, 3 years): "physicians spend more than one-half of their workday, nearly 6 hours, interacting with the EHR" (5.9 of 11.4 h; admin 44.2%; inbox 23.7%).
TIME-03 (B): Ratwani et al., JAMIA 2018, DOI 10.1093/jamia/ocy088 (4 systems): "an average of a nine-fold difference in time and eightfold difference in clicks" for the same tasks.
TIME-04 (B): Lew et al., JGIM 2025, DOI 10.1007/s11606-025-09466-x (3,996 clinicians): message volume "associated with a 25.5 min increase in EHR time" and +18.1 chart switches daily.
TIME-05 (B): Xia et al., JMIR 2025, DOI 10.2196/72768 (3,232 clinicians): ≥4 concurrent conversations ≈ +54.8 min, +41.6 switches; trainees +82.3 min. "Higher levels of conversational multitasking were associated with increased EHR time and more patient switches".
TIME-06 (C): Chi et al., JAMA Netw Open 2021, DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2021.17391 (n=12): "the AI system saved first-time physician users 18% of the time" (10.5 vs 12.8 min; accuracy no significant difference).
TIME-07 (C): Nolan et al., Appl Clin Inform 2017, DOI 10.4338/ACI-2017-06-RA-0110: ICU review median 9.2 min (IQR 7.3–14.7), 26.5 screens.
TIME-08 (C): Nunavut eConsult, Int J Circumpolar Health 2022, DOI 10.1080/22423982.2022.2151551: "eConsults took an average of 15.4 minutes of specialist time to complete"; 1.4-day response; 62% avoided in-person consult.
Workflow card: WHAT CHANGED (facts) / WHY IT MATTERS / NEXT WORKFLOW / SOURCES; provenance on demand; batch nonurgent; no parallel free-form chat channel.

WORKSTREAM 5 — Cost bases. Bottom line: constructs differ (reimbursement, claim cost, incremental inpatient cost, opportunity cost, modeled cost-effectiveness, pre/post savings); never sum without normalization; never present avoided cost-of-illness as achievable savings.
COST-01 (C): Spanish oncology coding (Ruiz-García et al., Clin Nutr 2022, DOI 10.1016/j.clnu.2021.11.028), n=266: "The cost reimbursement for each admission increased an average of 719.32€." Spain, euros, price-year UNVERIFIED.
COST-02 (B): Medicare cancer malnutrition (Sulo et al., J Nutr Health Aging 2022, DOI 10.1007/s12603-022-1826-4), >2.8M claims: ED cost $10,724 vs $4,935 (2018 USD); "such patients incurred more than 2-fold higher mean ED claim costs".
COST-03 (B): National AKI cost (Silver et al., J Hosp Med 2017, DOI 10.12788/jhm.2683), 29.76M hospitalizations: adjusted incremental $1,795 (1,692–1,899, 2012 basis), +1.1 days; dialysis-AKI +$11,016, +3.9 days. "AKI is associated with excess hospitalization costs and prolonged LOS."
COST-04 (B): AKI after coronary procedures (Prasad et al., Cathet Cardiovasc Interv 2020, DOI 10.1002/ccd.28824), n=2.76M: index +$8,416; "Overall excess healthcare burden associated with AKI was $1.67 billion." Dollar-year UNVERIFIED.
COST-05 (C): Modeled nutrition economics (Schuetz et al., BMJ Open 2021, DOI 10.1136/bmjopen-2020-046402), 27 trials n=6,803: "The estimated daily per-patient cost for in-hospital nutrition was US$6.23."; modeled saving $2,818/patient — MODELED, not observed.
COST-06 (C): Home-health QI (Riley et al., JPEN 2020, DOI 10.1002/jpen.1606): nonrandomized; program cost $261/patient; "Total cost savings from reduced 90-day healthcare resource utilization was $2,318,894, or $1500 per patient treated." Mixed 2013/2016/2017 dollars.
COST-07 (C): VA no-shows (Kheirkhah et al., BMC Health Serv Res 2016, DOI 10.1186/s12913-015-1243-z): "The average cost of no-show per patient was $196 in 2008."
COST-08 (C): Palliative reminder QI (Lagman et al., Am J Hosp Palliat Med 2021, DOI 10.1177/1049909120952322): no-show 11.8%→6.9%; "equivalent to an annual savings of about $79,200." 2016 USD opportunity basis.
Display rule: four separate lanes (PROGRAM COST / CAPACITY OR OPPORTUNITY VALUE / COST-OF-ILLNESS ASSOCIATION / REIMBURSEMENT); never sum lanes; never claim avoided AKI cost as savings absent prospective causal evidence.
